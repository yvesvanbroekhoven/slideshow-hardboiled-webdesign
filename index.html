So.. what's up with him?

Laws, rules & conventions matter, of course, but sometimes they get in the way of justice being served

Hardboiled webdesign is...

  • Never compromising on creating the best work we can for the web
  • Never be afraid to push boundaries
  • Break rules or invent new ones
  • Never hesitate to make the most of new technologies

The web laws

  • W3C
  • progressive enhancement
  • graceful degradation
  • cross browser compatible
  • indeable by search engines
  • ...

But the world's far from perfect

Best practices are only really recommendations

W3C is not a legal entity, so they can't force browser vendors & web professionals to adopt these standards & best practices, other then peer pressure and common sense.

We're fortunate that support for standards in browsers is now more complete and consistent than at any time past!

But there is still trouble in paradise

Deal with the older and less capable

It is standard practice to fight hard to create a website that looked & worked the same across all browsers, no matter their capabilities.

Hm.. That's not really hardboiled..

This isn't the way to build a better web

  • it holds us back
  • it forces us to make excuses for not doing what we know is the right thing

But our clients?! We have to do what they expect!

It is possible to give our clients what they want and at the same time use new technologies

That's what hardboiled webdesign is about

The web scene changed a lot the last 4 years!

  • IE's market share dipped
  • Rival browsers gained market share
  • mobile market is expanding faster than imagined

Instead of focusing on what we can do, most of us focus on what we can't

Instead of embracing the possible, most of us complain about limitations

Instead of getting excited, most of us whine and moan

Rewind to...

Hardboiled? I don't think so...

Don't feel bad, it's years of living with conventions about what's right & wrong.

But it's time to move forward, rethinking these conventions and clear up popular conventions like

  • websites should look the same in every browser
  • it's not safe to adopt new technologies until every browser supports them

The ol' religion

Progressive enhancement

Progressive enhancement is an approach to web design that builds documents for the least capable devices first, then moves on to enhance those documents... To allow a richer experience for those users with modern graphical browser software.

Actually this approach is holding back both our creative potential and the wider use of new technologies

Btw did you know this approach was recommended in

2003!

2003 was the year of:

  • massive iPod 30gb
  • OSX 10.2 (Jaguar)
  • Windows XP SP2
  • Photoshop CS
  • Dreamweaver 7
  • Frontpage 2003
  • IE6
  • Safari 1
  • ...

Why do we accept from software that time marches on and upgrades are both necessary and desirable?

While we stick to a received wisdom that's now more than 8 years old (and that's long in web years!)

Don't throw away everything

  • basic content and functionality should be accessible to all browsers
  • lean, clean, semantic HTML
  • CSS should acomplish all aspects of visual design
  • behaviour should be enabled using unobtrusive scripting

Maybe we could turn progressive enhancement into progressive enrichment?

We should treat these visual details as rewards for the browsers that support the advanced code that creates them. We're visually rewarding users of browsers that are forward thinking. That's the core of the term progressive enrichment

But then again, we're still starting at the bottom, with the lowest common design for less capable browsers.

No wonder we're stuck at border radius & dropshadow!

Let's make a U-turn and start from the top down and design for the best browsers first!

But but but, what about that poor less capable browsers?

The answer is consider how each progressively less capable browser will render a design

And instead of hacking our HTML, CSS & javascript to attempt cross-browser pixel perfection, embrace the differences and design around them.

Hey but isn't that graceful degradation?

Graceful degradation ensures that when styles & scripts are not available or understood, the content will remain accessible.

Considering accessibility and how websites function in older browsers is a fundamentally important part of what we do. But the term gracefull degration, as traditionally applied to visual design, implies that we should compromise.

To hell with being graceful!

Let's push graceful degradation further!

This means that designs will look different, sometimes very different, across browsers

So what's stopping us?

Nothing more than a few old-fashioned ideas..

Don't apply these principles as religions, but as starting points

It doesn't have to look the same

one preconception that often prevents us and our clients from making the most of emerging technologies

Do websites need to look exactly the same in every browser?

We need to step back from our endless battle to make it look the same across all platforms. We can't make our site look the same on a PDA as a 27" monitor. We can't make our site the same for someone on a speaking browser, and although things are improving there are still differences in support and implementation of various W3C standards. Let go, it's not going to look the same.
That quote was writtin in 2002..

Why are we still having the same conversation today?

Of course, some people still think that websites need to look and be experienced exactly the same in every browser, but those people probably still print their emails.

Web browsers have different capabilities and so websites can't look or be experienced the same in every one. Period.

Sure, we could spend time bulking up our pages with presentational markup, extra images and javascript workarounds. But that's precisely what we've been doing for years and it's often what clients expect

But it's up to us the make clear the realities of today's wide-ranging browser landscape.

We could limit our design to a lowest common browser, but when our users carry iPads and smartphones, will they really expect their experience to be the same on those as in IE7?

Print != Web

While print is fixed and limited, the web isn't! We must accept the ebb and flow of things.

Designs cannot look the same in all browsers because no 2 browsers are alike

No 2 browsers are alike because no 2 browser vendors are alike.

Clients might want a website to look and be experienced exactly the same, but we must remind them

politely but firmly

that a browser's capabilities are a browser maker's business, not ours.

Big brands have quality teams to ensure everything looks exactly the same because otherwise it could damage there image

But it's our job to convince them that differences create opportunities to demonstrate creativity and therefore should be embraced

Let's shift focus from looking backwards to looking forwards

invest time in design instead of lenghty workarounds

our clients will see real improvements in brand experience

Ask a client

Would you prefer me to spend my time hacking around issues for older browsers like IE7 or spend it instead on making the website look the best that it can on better desktop browser, as well as on the whole host of mobile browsers?

If a client reaise the issue that a design looks different in an alternative browser, never be defensive.

Explain that designing a range of tailored experiences will be better for everyone.

This helps clients understand the positive impact of browser differences rather than seeing them as imperfections

It's easier nowadays

With the rising of smartphones and tablets, clients already know about the different browsing experiences.

How to cope with older browsers?

  • Show warnings
  • Trick them in upgrading
  • write remedial stylesheets
  • ...

Often, we create our own problems

  • How many clients expect pixel precision?
  • Show static visuals and set unrealistic expectations

Clients often make the wrong assumptions. Sometimes they demand cross-browser pixel precision because they think it's the norm, not because their business needs depend on it.

Our job isn't to re-educate them, but ensure that their demands are based on real needs, facts & statistics.

Statistics tell us what browsers people are using today

But it's just as important to use these statistics to predict what browsers people will use in three, six, or maybe 12 months.

Investigate these statistics for your clients on the use of smartphones and modern browsers

They will understand that time is better spent on designing for the best browsers as they see that the numbers go up towards the future, and go down for less capable browsers.

Give them the choice

  • spend 3 days writing medial stylesheets
  • spend 1 day implementing and testing javascript solutions, and 2 days polishing design for better browsers
  • spend 10 minutes implementing a simple typography stylesheet for less capable browsers, and 3 days designing awesome edging ideas for better browsers

Being confident can be tough

But remember, our clients don't pay us just for showing up, they pay us for our knowledge & experience, and they want us to lead them in the right direction!

The contract

The landscape of web browsers and devices changes regularly and our approah is to look forward, not back. With that in mind we will test all our markup and CSS in current versions of all major desktop browsers to ensure that we make the most of them. Users of old or less capable browsers or devices will experience a design that is appropriate to the capabilities of their software.
